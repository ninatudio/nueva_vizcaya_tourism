Local Cuisine

What to Eat in Nueva Vizcaya

Nueva Vizcaya's food culture is shaped by its indigenous heritage and highland agriculture. From hearty Ilocano-influenced stews to fresh citrus produce, every meal tells a story.

Bagnet

Bagnet is deep-fried crispy pork belly — the Ilocano version of lechon kawali, and a beloved staple throughout Nueva Vizcaya. Pork belly is first boiled in seasoned water until tender, then deep-fried twice in oil until the skin becomes golden, blistered, and irresistibly crispy. It is typically served with a dipping sauce of vinegar, garlic, onion, and bagoong (shrimp paste), and paired with steamed rice or as a topping for pinakbet.

📌 Best paired with: Pinakbet, steamed white rice, and vinegar-garlic dip

🍴 Where to Try: Carinderia restaurants along Maharlika Highway in Solano and Bayombong; almost every local eatery in the province serves this dish.
Inabraw (Dinengdeng)

Inabraw (also called Dinengdeng in Ilocano) is a light, nutritious vegetable stew that is a cornerstone of indigenous cuisine in Nueva Vizcaya. It is made by simmering a variety of fresh local vegetables — such as ampalaya (bitter gourd), eggplant, squash, string beans, and dahon ng sili (chili leaves) — in a broth flavored primarily with bagoong isda (fermented fish paste). The dish is prized for its simplicity, fresh flavors, and health benefits.

📌 Best paired with: Fried fish (tilapia or bangus), bagnet, and steamed rice

🍴 Where to Try: Traditional eateries and carinderias in Bayombong, Solano, and Aritao. Often cooked at home using vegetables from backyard gardens.
Abal-Abal

Abal-Abal is a traditional indigenous delicacy of the Bugkalot (Ilongot) people — crispy deep-fried flying termites (winged termites), typically harvested during the early rainy season when they swarm after the first heavy rains. They are considered a high-protein seasonal delicacy and are fried with minimal seasoning to preserve their natural nutty flavor. Though unusual to outsiders, abal-abal is a cherished traditional food and a point of cultural pride for indigenous communities.

📌 Season: Early rainy season (May–June, after first rains)

🍴 Where to Try: Indigenous communities in the eastern municipalities; occasionally available at local markets in Kasibu and Alfonso Castañeda during peak season.
Pinikpikan

Pinikpikan is a traditional Cordilleran chicken dish with deep cultural and ceremonial roots among the indigenous highland communities of Nueva Vizcaya, shared with neighboring Benguet, Mountain Province, and Ifugao. The chicken is prepared using a traditional method and then cooked in a broth with etag (smoked, salted pork), vegetables, and sometimes ginger. The result is a richly flavored, aromatic soup with tender chicken and a distinctive smoky depth from the etag.

📌 Cultural note: Traditionally prepared during rituals and celebrations in Kalanguya and Kankana-ey communities

🍴 Where to Try: Highland municipalities of Kayapa, Ambaguio, and Santa Fe; some specialty restaurants in Bayombong serve this dish.
Highland Citrus Fruits

Nueva Vizcaya is officially recognized as the Citrus Capital of the Philippines, and for good reason. The cool highland climate of Kayapa, Ambaguio, and Santa Fe produces exceptionally sweet and juicy citrus fruits including ponkan (mandarin orange), dalandan (Philippine orange), and suha (pomelo). These fruits are sweeter and more aromatic than lowland varieties, and are sold fresh, as juices, candies, and jams throughout the province and exported to Metro Manila.

📌 Peak season: November to February

🍴 Where to Try: Directly from farms in Kayapa and Ambaguio; roadside stalls along Maharlika Highway; pasalubong centers in Bayombong and Solano.
Pinakbet

Pinakbet is a classic Ilocano vegetable dish widely eaten throughout Nueva Vizcaya. It consists of a colorful medley of local vegetables — bitter gourd, eggplant, okra, squash, string beans, and tomatoes — stewed with bagoong (shrimp or fish paste) and often topped with crispy bagnet or lechon. The dish showcases the province's abundance of fresh highland produce and is a testament to the Ilocano tradition of healthy, vegetable-forward cooking.

📌 Best paired with: Steamed rice and bagnet

🍴 Where to Try: Any local eatery or carinderia throughout the province. Every municipality has its own version of pinakbet.

Pasalubong to Bring Home

🍊
Fresh Citrus Fruits

Ponkan, dalandan, and suha from highland farms. Available November–February at roadside stands and pasalubong shops throughout the province.

🍬
Citrus Candies & Jams

Locally produced ponkan candies, marmalades, and citrus jams are popular souvenirs. Look for them at the Bayombong Public Market and pasalubong centers in Solano.

🧵
Woven Handicrafts

Handwoven baskets, textiles, and bags created by indigenous artisans are beautiful and culturally significant souvenirs unique to Nueva Vizcaya.
